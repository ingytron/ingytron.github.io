A selection of mini-essays about myself

Background

I was a computer fix-it kid, I read computer manuals and tweaked DOS to improve game performance (img: I got that snake to go so fast that we couldn't play it. I helped my Dad recover his work computer after my sister accidentally ran a deletion macro (it was a single keystroke!). My computers teacher ran out of things to do and so started me writing code in BASIC, later Pascal. I did a tertiary course in computing during my high school years, and another at night school but it was rather boring and basic, and I even ended up teaching the class at one point (I was 17 at the time, but the instructor needed some help with using Excel) I got a job at Compaq right from school and worked my way up through tech support. I later worked as a systems administrator and datacentre engineer, working on mission-critical systems, mostly linux and Windows servers. I moved to Canada to work briefly for Fortinet, doing technical support for their firewall products (configuration, VPN connectivity, e-mail scanning) and was then headhunted by IBM. Unfortunately, the thrill was gone and I found that my heart just wasn't in it anymore. I needed a change and decided to pursue my interest in web design instead.

I can servers and design networks. I'm very technically curious and always know how things work and how we can better solve problems.

I wanted design to be enough, but it turns out that I'm highly picky about how code should be written, and I just preferred to do it myself rather than hand over everything to a developer, plus I have been freelancing since before my first child was born. I found that doing everything myself and also trying to attract clients, who had deadlines, was too much to ask of myself while I had small kids, so I took a break from working for clients and have just been doing a lot of coding and tutorials.

Get with the programming

I started out with HTML4 and CSS, but I wasn't really good at programming because I think I was trying to learn JavaScript and it just really never came together for me. It was hard to read and the syntax drove me crazy because I was always getting the parentheses and semi-colons wrong. I built myself an online store, which I built with osCommerce. I figured out how to style it, fairly crudely, and my friend who is a graphic designer and artist did a logo for me. It was marginally profitable but I decided it wasn't really what I wanted to be doing. This was really my first brush with a content management system, and they sure did make life easier. I found WordPress started to build sites using it, theming, more theming. I tried working with WooThemes but I just wanted more flexibility. I worked with Drupal for a while too, but it wasn't as slick as WordPress, and overkill for most sites anybody ever asked me to build (which invariably consisted of a home page, an about page, a contact page and make sure the address is on there somewhere too). I worked more with WordPress and the Genesis Theme Framework. I tried (and was not impressed by) Joomla, and also Magento. I was hacking away at PHP to do things and the syntax was still awful, but it worked.

Building sites for people and getting them to pay you adequately for it is a lot more challenging than I thought it would be. I desire stability and work/life balance.

I built quite a lot of websites for friends with just HTML and CSS, but I wanted to know how to build sites which looked a little better, I googled extensively and found that most of the sites that I liked were designed by graphic designers. Armed with that knowledge, I went to graphic design school. I learned the fundamentals of design (which is something I wanted to learn). I did PHP training, I knew SQL quite well from my work doing system administration, but then I discovered Ruby on Rails and it really let me build something to my own specification, without worrying about having to use 15 plugins and security scanning issues that you get with WordPress, its ubiquity is also its own curse.

If you have read this far, I owe you a cookie.

The other thing is that I've learned to stop worrying and love the JavaScript. It's about a zillion times better these days because online training is fantastic and instant feedback is far better than having to copy code out of a manual and then later read it was a printing error (so frustrating!). Anyhow, that kind of snowballed and I've been taking courses on Python development, algorithms, and mostly I've just been trying to do a bunch of practice and writing a lot of code for various projects that I've been working on whilst I took a break for mat leave. My plan was to take some time off and build out some projects in WordPress but finding how to build the right solution using WordPress turned into WordPress isn't the be-all and end-all of doing things and the projects that I was trying to build were a better fit for being built in RoR, so that's what I set out to do instead.

Writing code is perfect for spelling and punctuation enthusiasts, such as myself. Although code may be poetry, not all code is poetry — and as with prose — it's always a good idea to copyedit & proofread it. I will use as many ampersands as humanly possible because it's my favourite punctuation mark.
